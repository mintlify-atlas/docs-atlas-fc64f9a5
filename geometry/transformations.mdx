Geometric transformations map points from one coordinate frame to another. Representing them as **homogeneous matrices** allows any chain of transformations to be composed by matrix multiplication, and makes the hierarchy of transformation groups explicit.

## Hierarchy of 2D transformations

Each class of 2D transformation preserves a strictly smaller set of geometric properties than the one below it.

| Class | DOF | Preserved | Matrix form |
|---|---|---|---|
| Translation | 2 | Distances, angles | $\begin{pmatrix}I & \mathbf{t}\\ \mathbf{0}^\top & 1\end{pmatrix}$ |
| Euclidean (rigid) | 3 | Distances, angles | $\begin{pmatrix}R & \mathbf{t}\\ \mathbf{0}^\top & 1\end{pmatrix}$ |
| Similarity | 4 | Angles, ratios of lengths | $\begin{pmatrix}sR & \mathbf{t}\\ \mathbf{0}^\top & 1\end{pmatrix}$ |
| Affine | 6 | Parallelism, area ratios | $\begin{pmatrix}A & \mathbf{t}\\ \mathbf{0}^\top & 1\end{pmatrix}$ |
| Projective (homography) | 8 | Cross-ratio, collinearity | $\begin{pmatrix}\tilde{A} & \tilde{\mathbf{t}}\\ \mathbf{v}^\top & v\end{pmatrix}$ |

### Translation

$$
\mathbf{m}' = T\,\mathbf{m}, \qquad T = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & t_x\\ 0 & 1 & t_y\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$

### Rotation by angle $\theta$

$$
R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}
$$

The full rigid-body matrix in homogeneous form:

$$
H_{\text{Eucl}} = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & t_x\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & t_y\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$

### Affine transformation

$$
H_{\text{aff}} = \begin{pmatrix}a_{11} & a_{12} & t_x\\ a_{21} & a_{22} & t_y\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$

Parallel lines remain parallel under affine maps; angles and lengths in general do not.

### Projective (homography)

$$
H = \begin{pmatrix}h_{11} & h_{12} & h_{13}\\ h_{21} & h_{22} & h_{23}\\ h_{31} & h_{32} & h_{33}\end{pmatrix}
$$

defined up to scale (8 degrees of freedom). Straight lines map to straight lines, but parallelism is not preserved.

## 3D transformations

In 3D the same hierarchy applies. A rigid-body transformation uses a $3\times3$ rotation matrix $R$ and a translation vector $\mathbf{t}\in\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$
\mathbf{M}' = \begin{pmatrix}R & \mathbf{t}\\ \mathbf{0}^\top & 1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{M}
$$

### Rotation matrices and the Rodrigues formula

Any 3D rotation can be parameterised by an axis $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ (unit vector) and an angle $\theta$. The **Rodrigues rotation formula** gives:

$$
R = I\cos\theta + (1-\cos\theta)\hat{\mathbf{n}}\hat{\mathbf{n}}^\top + \sin\theta\,[\hat{\mathbf{n}}]_\times
$$

where $[\hat{\mathbf{n}}]_\times$ is the skew-symmetric cross-product matrix:

$$
[\mathbf{n}]_\times = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -n_z & n_y\\ n_z & 0 & -n_x\\ -n_y & n_x & 0\end{pmatrix}
$$

In MATLAB, the compact **Rodrigues vector** $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \theta\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ encodes both axis and angle in 3 numbers, and the conversion is available via `rodrig2R` / `R2rodrig`.

## 3D-to-2D perspective projection

The full **camera projection matrix** $P$ maps a 3D world point $\mathbf{M}$ to a 2D image point $\mathbf{m}$:

$$
\mathbf{m} \sim P\,\mathbf{M}, \qquad P = K\,[R\;|\;\mathbf{t}]
$$

where $K$ is the intrinsic matrix (see [Camera Calibration](/geometry/camera-calibration)):

$$
K = \begin{pmatrix}f_x & s & c_x\\ 0 & f_y & c_y\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$

```matlab 3D pyramid transformations (3DPyramid)
% 3D representation of a Pyramid — apply rotation and translation
% (c) Domingo Mery (2014)

% Define 5 pyramid vertices in homogeneous coordinates
p1 = [0 0 0 1]';
p2 = [2 0 0 1]';
p3 = [2 2 0 1]';
p4 = [0 2 0 1]';
p5 = [1 1 2 1]';  % apex
P  = [p1 p2 p3 p4 p5];

% Connectivity (draw edges between vertices)
s = [1 2 5 1 4 5 3 2 3 4];
M = P(:, s);

% Translation t and Rodrigues rotation vector w
t = [1 2 3]';  w = [0 0 0]';
CV02_PlotMesh3D(M, t, w);

% Rotate 90 degrees around x-axis
t = [0 0 0]';  w = [pi/2 0 0]';
CV02_PlotMesh3D(M, t, w);

% Animate full rotation around each axis
for wx = 0:pi/36:2*pi
    w(1) = wx;
    CV02_PlotMesh3D(M, t, w);
    drawnow
end
```

## Key properties at a glance

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Composing transformations">
    Because every transformation is a matrix, composing two transformations is just matrix multiplication: $H_{\text{total}} = H_2 H_1$. The order matters — matrix multiplication is not commutative in general.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Inverting a rigid-body transformation">
    For a rigid-body matrix $(R,\mathbf{t})$ the inverse is $(R^\top, -R^\top\mathbf{t})$, since rotation matrices are orthogonal ($R^{-1} = R^\top$). This is much cheaper than a general matrix inversion.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Degrees of freedom count">
    Translation: 2 (tx, ty). Euclidean: 3 (+angle). Similarity: 4 (+scale). Affine: 6 (full 2×2 matrix + translation). Projective: 8 (3×3 matrix up to scale). In 3D, rigid-body has 6 DOF (3 rotation + 3 translation).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What is preserved under each transformation?">
    Euclidean: distances and angles. Similarity: angles and length ratios. Affine: parallelism, ratios of collinear lengths, area ratios. Projective: cross-ratio of four collinear points, and collinearity.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
